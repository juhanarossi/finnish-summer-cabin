The summer cabin culture truly began to take root and expand in the post-World War II era due to a variety of factors. First, Finland transitioned from being an agrarian society to a more urban one. As a result, most people living in the cities were first-generation urban dwellers with close links to the countryside. Hence, they had a motive to visit the countryside and their former homes regularly. Due to these links, they could either obtain or inherit lakeside parcels of land suitable for building a summer cabin.
Furthermore, the post-World War II era saw a steady rise in the standard of living and an increase in the leisure time available to wage earners. Consequently, more and more people had the means and time to set up a summer place. As the term “summer cabin” suggests, these structures were simple and basic, intended primarily for summer use. They often consisted of just one or two rooms of living space, a small kitchen, a wood-burning stove for heating, and a sauna for bathing and washing.
